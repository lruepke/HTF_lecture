Exercise 1¶

Goals

  • explore FVM theory

  • link FVM theory and OpenFOAM solver implementation

  • step by step exploring details of the solver and solving process

Step 1: Geometric and physical modeling¶

(27)¶\[\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot D \nabla T\]

Here \(D\) is the thermal diffusivity, can be a constant value. see also Theory

What we need to solve ?

Temperature field \(T\) of the model region at time \(t\), therefore \(T\) is the only unknown variable of our problem.

Step 3: Equation discretization¶

The equation discretization step is performed over each element of the computational domain to yield an algebraic relation that connects the value of a variable in an element to the values of the variable in the neighboring elements [Moukalled et al., 2016].

Goal

Transform partial differential equation (27) into a set of algebraic equations: \(\mathbf{A}[T] = \mathbf{b}\).

  1. Integrate equation (27) over element \(C\) (the green cell in Fig. 34) that enables recovering its integral balance form as,

(28)¶\[\iiint\limits_{V_C} \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} dV \equiv b_C = \iiint\limits_{V_C} \nabla \cdot D\nabla T dV\]

Note that the transient term \(\iiint\limits_{V_C} \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} dV \equiv b_C\) will be processed in the later section

  1. Transform the volume integral on the right hand side of equation (28) into a surface integral by applying the divergence theorem,

(29)¶\[b_C = \iint\limits_{S_C} (D\nabla T)\cdot d\vec{S}\]

The equation (29) is actually a heat balance over cell \(C\). It is basically the integral form of the original partial differential equation and involves no approximation.

  1. Transform the surface integral in equation (29) as a summation over the control volume faces (here introduce the first approximation),

(30)¶\[b_C = \sum\limits_{f\sim nb(C)} (D\nabla T)_f\cdot \vec{S}_f\]

Here \(f\) denotes the integration point at the centroid of the bounding face. For a specific cell \(C\) shown in Fig. 33, the equation (30) can be expanded as,

(31)¶\[b_C = \color{red}{(D\nabla T)_{f_1} \cdot \vec{S}_{f_1}} + (D\nabla T)_{f_2} \cdot \vec{S}_{f_2} + (D\nabla T)_{f_3} \cdot \vec{S}_{f_3}+ (D\nabla T)_{f_4} \cdot \vec{S}_{f_4}\]

  1. Evaluating gradient at centroid of faces

    4.1 Considering face \(f_1\), calculate the first term on the right hand side of the equation (31),

    (32)¶\[\color{red}{(D\nabla T)_{f_1} \cdot \vec{S}_{f_1}} = \left(D \frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \vec{i} + D\frac{\partial T}{\partial y} \vec{j} \right)_{f_1} \cdot \Delta y_{f_1} \vec{i} = \color{blue}{\left( D\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \right)_{f_1} \Delta y_{f_1}}\]

    where

    • \(S_{f_1} = \Delta y_{f_1}\) is the area of face \(f_1\) by assuming \(\Delta z = 1\).

    • \(\vec{S}_{f_1} = S_{f_1} \vec{n}_{f_1}\) is the surface vector of face \(f_1\).

    • \(\vec{n}_{f_1} = \vec{i}\) is the normal vector of the face \(f_1\) directed out of the cell \(C\) (see Fig. 34).

    4.2 Calculate gradient of \(T\) at face centroid, here introduce the second approximation, e.g. assuming linear variation of T and then the gradient term in the equation (32) can be approximated as,

    \[\color{blue}{\left( D\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \right)_{f_1} \Delta y_{f_1}} = D\frac{T_{F_1} - T_{C}}{x_{F_1} - x_C}\Delta y_{f_1} = D\frac{T_{F_1} - T_{C}}{\delta x_{f_1}}\Delta y_{f_1} = \color{green}{a_{F_1}} (T_{F_1} - T_C)\]

    where \(\delta x_{f_1}\) represents the distance between cell \(C\) and \(F_1\).

    4.3 Do the same thing for the remain faces (\(f_2, f_3, f_4\)) and get their coefficients \(\color{green}{a_{F_2}, a_{F_3}, a_{F_4}}\) respectively,

    (33)¶\[\color{green}{a_{F_1}} = \frac{\Delta y_{f_1}}{\delta x_{f_1}}, \color{green}{a_{F_2}} = \frac{\Delta x_{f_2}}{\delta y_{f_2}}, \color{green}{a_{F_3}} = \frac{\Delta x_{f_3}}{\delta y_{f_3}}, \color{green}{a_{F_4}} = \frac{\Delta y_{f_4}}{\delta x_{f_4}}\]

  2. Construct matrix form of the equation (30) by substituting the above relations,

(34)¶\[\begin{split}\begin{eqnarray} \sum\limits_{f\sim nb(C)} (D\nabla T)_f\cdot \vec{S}_f & = & \sum\limits_{F\sim NB(C)} a_F(T_F - T_C) = \sum\limits_{F\sim NB(C)} a_F T_F - T_C\sum\limits_{F\sim NB(C)} a_F \\ & = & a_CT_C + \sum\limits_{F\sim NB(C)} a_F T_F \end{eqnarray}\end{split}\]

Considering \(b_C\) matrix form of the equation (30) can be written as,

(35)¶\[\mathbf{A}_{N \times N} \mathbf{T}_{N\times1} = \mathbf{b}_{N\times1}\]

here \(N\) is the number of cell, \(\mathbf{A}\) is a sparse matrix of coefficients, \(\mathbf{T}\) is cell-centered temperature field and \(\mathbf{b}\) is the transient term which will explained in the later section. For the steady state problem, \(\mathbf{b} \equiv 0\). The matrix is visualized as Fig. 35.

Fig. 35 Visualization of \(\mathbf{A}_{N \times N} \mathbf{T}_{N\times1} = \mathbf{b}_{N\times1}\). The coefficients of the selected cell \(C\) (Fig. 34) are marked by green rectangle¶
